# 002 — Navigation Philosophy

What "navigating a dynamic galaxy" actually means for UX.

---

## Stars are images, clusters are shoots/themes

Not individual nodes floating independently in space, but gravitationally-bound groups with clear identity. A cluster of same-session images is a nebula — dense, coherent, visually unified. A thematic thread across sessions is a constellation — looser, connected by visible lines, spanning distance.

The galaxy isn't a random scattering of points. It has structure. That structure *is* the navigation.

## The hero is your spacecraft

When you anchor an image, you're not "selecting an item from a list." You're orbiting that star. Your viewport is centered on its neighborhood. Nearby clusters are visible and reachable. Distant ones are hinted at by their light — a glow on the horizon, a faint cluster in the periphery.

The anchor image isn't highlighted in a grid. It *is* where you are. Everything else is positioned relative to it.

## Navigation is travel, not selection

Moving between images should feel like *journeying* through space. Clicking a neighbor isn't "selecting the next item" — it's traveling to a new vantage point. The transition should communicate distance: nearby images in the same cluster = a short hop. An image in a different session cluster = a longer journey with visible traversal.

Back button = retracing your path, not "undo." The history trail is your flight path through the galaxy.

## Information as starlight

Text, labels, and navigation cues should glow from within the content, not overlay it from outside. A cluster's label should float near it like a nebula's name on a star chart — part of the scene, not a tooltip bolted on.

Metadata isn't chrome applied to the experience. It's light emitted by the content itself. Dates, tags, technical data — these radiate outward from the image, visible when you're close enough, fading as you move away.

HUD-style, not chrome-style. The difference: chrome belongs to the application frame. HUD belongs to the world you're navigating.

## The whole screen breathes

The background isn't a static dark canvas. It shifts color with the neighborhood palette — warm amber when surrounded by golden hour images, cool blue-grey near overcast street photography, deep black near high-contrast studio work.

Particle density changes with cluster richness. A dense region of the gallery produces visible activity — subtle motion, gentle drift. Sparse regions are calm, open, quiet.

Ambient motion reflects the mood of the current neighborhood. The screen is never truly still, but it's never distractingly busy. It breathes.

## Clusters have gravity

Same-session images should visually *cohere* as a tight group. Not spread across rings or scattered by force repulsion, but bound together like a planetary system. They orbit each other, maintaining proximity, presenting as a unit.

Theme clusters are looser — a constellation, not a planet. The images are connected by visible threads of shared meaning, but they maintain individual identity and spacing.

Color neighborhoods are atmospheric, not structural. They don't create hard groups — they create *zones*. The warm corner of the galaxy. The high-contrast region. You drift through these zones, and the atmosphere changes.

## Entry is arrival

Opening the app isn't "loading a page." It's arriving in the galaxy. The very first frame should communicate:
- There is a rich, structured world here
- It has regions, clusters, density variations
- You can go anywhere
- Everything is connected to everything else

No loading screens, no "select an album," no empty states. You land in the middle of it. The galaxy was here before you arrived. You're exploring, not initializing.
